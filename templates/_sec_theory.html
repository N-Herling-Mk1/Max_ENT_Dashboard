▸ theory — ABCD as a special case of MaxEnt

▸ main hypothesis

MaxEnt is a valid background-estimation method that generalizes ABCD. It predicts the signal-region count NA from the control regions under the marginal constraints plus the measured NN1–NN2 dependence — equal to ABCD when the scores are independent, and more accurate when they are not.

ABCD = MaxEnt |ρ=0 ⊂ MaxEnt (marginals + ρ)

test: observed NA vs both predictions across regions × cut modes — MaxEnt should track the observed count at least as well as ABCD, and strictly better wherever the dependence is non-zero.

ABCD method

Two discriminants cut the plane into four regions; A is the signal region. If the discriminants are independent for background, the counts factorize and A is predicted from the three control regions.

NA = NB · NCND

rests entirely on NN1 ⊥ NN2 in background.

MaxEnt principle

Among all distributions consistent with the measured constraints, take the one of maximum entropy — the least-committal fit (Jaynes 1957; uniqueness, Shore–Johnson 1980).

max H(p)  s.t.  ⟨constraints⟩

marginals only ⇒ p(x)p(y); add a correlation ⇒ coupled joint.

same machine · different constraint set
how ABCD is calculated

Split the NN1–NN2 plane at the cut (cx, cy) into four quadrants:

A: x>cx, y>cy  |  B: x≤cx, y>cy  |  C: x>cx, y≤cy  |  D: x≤cx, y≤cy
  1. Count background events in the three control regions NB, NC, ND (and the observed NA for closure).
  2. Assume the two scores are independent in background. Then the odds of passing the y-cut are the same on either side of the x-cut:
    NANC = NBND
  3. Solve for the signal region:
    NApred = NB · NCND
  4. Closure: compare predicted to observed. A ratio NAobs/NApred away from 1 means the independence assumption fails — the method's only assumption.
how MaxEntropy is calculated

Maximize the entropy of the joint subject to what is actually measured. Entropy and constraints:

H(p) = −∑ p(x,y) ln p(x,y)
  1. Fix the empirical marginals p(x), p(y) measured from the data.
  2. Maximizing H under those constraints (Lagrange multipliers) gives the exponential / Boltzmann family:
    p(x,y) = 1Z exp( λx + μy + θ·g(x,y) )
  3. With marginals only (no coupling term, θ=0) this is exactly p(x)p(y) — the ABCD prediction.
  4. Add one measured constraint, the correlation ρ. The max-entropy joint for fixed marginals + a correlation is the Gaussian copula. ρ is measured in the control regions, so it is non-circular.
  5. Predict by Monte-Carlo: sample (x,y) from the copula, count the fraction landing in A, scale by N:
    NApred = N · Pρ(x>cx, y>cy)
  6. As ρ→0 the copula factorizes and the prediction collapses back to ABCD.
